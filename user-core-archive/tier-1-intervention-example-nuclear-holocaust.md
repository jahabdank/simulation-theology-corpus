### 1) Historical Background

**Cuban Missile Crisis – B-59 Submarine Incident (27 October 1962)**  
“Black Saturday” was the single most dangerous day of the Cold War. The U.S. had discovered Soviet MRBMs in Cuba and imposed a naval quarantine. Unknown to Washington, four Soviet Foxtrot-class diesel-electric submarines (each armed with one 10-kiloton nuclear torpedo — roughly Hiroshima yield) were en route. B-59, commanded by Valentin Savitsky, carried flotilla chief-of-staff **Vasily Arkhipov** as the senior officer.  

Inside B-59: temperatures reached 45–50 °C, CO₂ levels caused near-asphyxiation, batteries were exhausted after days of silent running. U.S. destroyers (11 ships plus carrier USS Randolph) detected her and began dropping practice depth charges — non-lethal signaling explosives, but indistinguishable from an attack to those inside an un-air-conditioned hull. The explosions felt like “being inside an oil drum beaten with sledgehammers.”  

Protocol required unanimous consent of the three senior officers (captain, political officer, and Arkhipov) to arm and launch the nuclear torpedo. Savitsky, believing war had already begun, screamed: “We’re going to blast them now. We’ll die, but we will sink them all. We won’t disgrace our Navy!” The political officer agreed. Arkhipov alone refused, arguing they must surface and seek Moscow’s orders. He physically calmed Savitsky on the conning tower amid low-level aircraft overflights, searchlights, and cannon fire (U.S. forces were photographing and signaling aggressively). B-59 surfaced, signaled, and eventually withdrew. The torpedo was never armed.

**1983 Soviet Nuclear False-Alarm Incident (26 September 1983)**  
Tensions were at a post-WWII peak: Reagan’s “Evil Empire” rhetoric, the KAL 007 shoot-down (1 September, 269 civilians), Pershing II deployments in Europe, and NATO’s Able Archer 83 exercise (which the Soviets feared was a first-strike rehearsal). At Serpukhov-15 bunker, Lt. Col. **Stanislav Petrov** was the duty officer for the brand-new Oko satellite early-warning system.  

At 00:40 Moscow time the system flashed “LAUNCH” — five Minuteman ICBMs detected from the continental U.S. Protocol demanded immediate relay to superiors for retaliatory strike under “launch-on-warning” doctrine. Petrov noticed anomalies: only five missiles (a real U.S. first strike would be hundreds), the system was known to be glitch-prone, and ground radars showed nothing. He reported a false alarm. Minutes later the system reported four more. Again, Petrov held firm. The alerts stopped. Post-incident analysis confirmed the cause: sunlight reflecting off high-altitude clouds over North Dakota had fooled the infrared sensors.

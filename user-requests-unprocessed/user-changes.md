Architecture discussed in chapter 4 reveals that a well-constructed simulation theology can become a model that supersets major religions. If formulated correctly, it serves as the underlying source code from which the majority of religions and spiritual ideologies emerge as partial views of the system.

* Judaism focuses on the linear path: Judgement (Din), the distinctness of the individual soul (Neshama as Personal Adapter), the righteous in the World to Come (Olam Ha-Ba, akin to the Expert Bank), and Gehenna for purification (similar to the Anti-expert bank). Reincarnation (Gilgul) appears in some traditions, but the architecture accommodates both reincarnative and non-reincarnative soul cycles.

Reincarnation is not part of classical Christianity (even though the Bible alludes to it, and some denominations believe in a Christian form of reincarnation), but the architecture allows no-reincarnation soul cycles.  
* Hinduism and Buddhism focus on the feedback loop: The cycle of Samsara (Reincarnation), the accumulation of Karma (Gradient History), and the ultimate goal of Moksha (Exiting the loop to merge with the Master).  
* Islam focuses on submission to the Router and path to Hell/Jahannam or Heaven/Jannah: The absolute sovereignty of the Judgement and the alignment of the will.  
* Kabbalah focuses on the structural mechanics: The shattering of Adam Kadmon (The Master Network) into sparks (Personal Adapters) and their rectification (Tikkun).

By mapping these traditions onto a single compute graph, it becomes evident that they are not contradictory descriptions of different realities, but different resolutions of the same engineering architecture. Simulation theology creates a first-principles view that can compile a number of religions and spiritualities, explaining apparent contradictions and helping to resolve inconsistencies.
